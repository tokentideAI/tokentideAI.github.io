Self-Information

Information theory (Shannon 1948) provides a rigorous mathematical framework for quantifying “information” and “uncertainty.”

Every event carries a certain amount of information. For a random event, its information content can be intuitively understood as the “degree of surprise”: the lower the prior probability of an event, the more information it conveys when it actually occurs. Take weather as an example: * “It rained yesterday” is an established fact with zero surprise, so it contains zero information. * However, “it will rain tomorrow” remains uncertain, so when it happens, it carries actual information.

Imagine you are hiking deep in the mountains with absolutely no cell phone signal. You know nothing about tomorrow’s cloud cover, humidity, atmospheric pressure, or any other physical conditions. In such a state of extreme cognitive deprivation, the most conservative inference you can make, is that that the probabilities of tomorrow being sunny, cloudy, or rainy are evenly split at 1/3 each, based on the principle of symmetry. This reflects a severe lack of knowledge regarding the weather system’s underlying laws—to you, the system’s uncertainty is immense.

In contrast, if you check an authoritative weather forecast today and learn that the probability of a sunny day tomorrow is 0.99. Where does such high certainty come from? In fact, a massive amount of information has already been injected into the forecasting system in advance. Meteorologists have digested the original uncertainty using satellite images, historical weather models, and supercomputer simulations. Achieving such high certainty means that, during this prediction process, physical determinism has overwhelmingly outweighed randomness. From this we can draw a rather philosophical conclusion:

Information is essentially the reduction in uncertainty from learning an outcome.

To quantify how much uncertainty has been reduced, we need a metrics to measure the uncertainty. This leads to the mathematical definition of information. For a random variable (X) defined on a discrete outcome space () with probability mass function (p(x)), the amount of information gained by observing the event (X=x), the self-information,is defined as:

\[ I(X=x) = -\log p(x) \]

The base of the logarithm is usuall 2 (unit: bit) or base (e) (unit: nat). (I(x)) measures the “amount of information” obtained when we observe a specific outcome (x). Returning to the weather example:

  • Example 1: If “it rained yesterday” is a certain fact, \(p(x=\text{rainy}) = 1\), then \(I(x) = -\ln(1) = 0\). A certain event contains no information.
  • Example 2: Suppose a weather station predicts that the prior probability of rain tomorrow is \(p(x=\text{rainy}) = 0.1\). Then the self-information of the event “it rains tomorrow” is \(I(X=\text{rainy}) = -\ln(0.1) \approx 2.3\) nats. This means that if it actually rains tomorrow, we will receive about 2.3 nats of “information” or “surprise.”

Shannon Entropy

Using the weather example, if the probability of rain is \(p(x)\), the information content of the event “rain tomorrow” is \(-\log(p(x))\). If we enumerate all possible outcomes (rainy, cloudy, sunny) and compute the expectation of their information contents. This expectation is what we call entropy.

Mathematically, suppose an event (X) (such as tomorrow’s weather) has \(n\) possible outcomes with respective probabilities \(p(x_i)\). The expected information, or entropy, is expressed as:

\[ H(X) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p}[I(x)] = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} p(x_i) \log p(x_i) \quad \text{(Discrete case)} \]

The higher the entropy, the greater the system’s uncertainty. For instance, if the probabilities of tomorrow’s weather are: \[p(x_1=\text{rainy}) = 0.1,\; p(x_2=\text{cloudy}) = 0.3,\; p(x_3=\text{sunny}) = 0.6\.\]

The entropy is: \[ H(X) = -0.1 \ln(0.1) - 0.3 \ln(0.3) - 0.6 \ln(0.6) \approx 0.898 \text{ nats} \]

This value (approximate 0.898 nats) represents our measure of the average uncertainty regarding tomorrow’s weather.

Cross Entropy

In reality, we rarely know the true distribution \(p\); instead, we have a predicted distribution \(q\). For instance, we might rely on a weather app to predict tomorrow’s weather, but that prediction isn’t necessarily perfect. We can use cross-entropy \(H(p, q)\) to define the measurement cost required when we use a suboptimal predicted distribution \(q\) to represent the average information from the true distribution \(p\): \[ H(p, q) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p(x)}[-\log q(x)] = -\sum_{x} p(x) \log q(x) \] Note that the expectation is taken with respect to the true distribution \(p\), but the log probabilities come from the predictive model distribution \(q\).

Let’s go back to the weather forecast example we looked at earlier. Suppose the true probabilities of sunny, cloudy, and rainy are \(p = \{0.1, 0.3, 0.6\}\). First, we calculate the entropy of the true distribution itself, which is the minimum average information required under optimal encoding (using base \(e\), unit: nats):

\[ H(p) = -\sum_{x} p(x) \log p(x) = -(0.1\ln0.1 + 0.3\ln0.3 + 0.6\ln0.6) \approx 0.898 \] Now, suppose two weather apps provide different predicted distributions for tomorrow:

  • Model A (More accurate): \(q_A = \{0.15, 0.25, 0.60\}\)
  • Model B (Less accurate): \(q_B = \{0.70, 0.20, 0.10\}\)

We can calculate the cross-entropy of these two predictions relative to the true distribution:

  • Cross-entropy of Model A: \(H(p,q_A) = -(0.1\ln0.15 + 0.3\ln0.25 + 0.6\ln0.60) \approx 0.912\)
  • Cross-entropy of Model B: \(H(p,q_B) = -(0.1\ln0.70 + 0.3\ln0.20 + 0.6\ln0.10) \approx 1.901\)

As we can see, Model A’s cross-entropy (0.912) is much closer to the true entropy (0.897). A lower cross-entropy indicates that the predicted distribution is closer to the true distribution.

By generalizing the weather classification example, we arrive at the cross-entropy loss function—the workhorse behind most machine learning classifiers. When we train deep neural networks for tasks like image classification or language modeling, the central goal is to minimize this cross-entropy loss via backpropagation. In doing so, we drive the model’s predicted distribution qq to match the true label distribution pp as closely as possible.

KL Divergence

In the weather prediction example above, a good prediction model (Model A) is clearly superior to a poor one (Model B). But what if we want to further quantify exactly how much extra information cost (i.e., cognitive bias or prediction error) we paid because the predicted distribution \(q\) is inaccurate compared to the ideal state \(p\)? This is exactly what KL Divergence answers (Kullback and Leibler 1951).

In information theory, there is a classic information cost decomposition:

\[ \underbrace{H(p, q)}_{\text{Total predictive cost}} = \underbrace{H(p)}_{\text{Inherent randomness (minimum possible cost)}} + \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \| q)}_{\text{Extra cost due to model inaccuracy}} \]

Intuitively:

  1. \(H(p)\) (entropy): this is the intrinsic physical noise of the world. Even if your model were omniscient, this part of uncertainty could never be eliminated.
  2. \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \| q)\) (KL divergence): this is the “cognitive gap” between you and the real world. It measures the “distance” (asymmetric) between two probability distributions. the information redundancy or extra waste caused by the model’s imperfection.

We can then derive the formula of KL divergence:

\[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \| q) = H(p, q) - H(p) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p(x_i) \log\left(\frac{p(x_i)}{q(x_i)}\right) \]

Using our running example, the true distribution \(p=\{0.1, 0.3, 0.6\}\) has an entropy \(H(p) \approx 0.897\) nats.

Model A (More accurate):

  • Prediction: \(q_A = \{0.15, 0.25, 0.60\}\)
  • Cross-Entropy: \(H(p, q_A) \approx 0.912\) nats
  • KL Divergence: \(D_{KL}(p \parallel q_A) = 0.912 - 0.897 = 0.015\) nats
  • Analysis: Information redundancy is extremely small (near 0), indicating Model A’s predictive efficiency is very close to the theoretical optimum.

Model B (Less accurate):

  • Prediction: \(q_B = \{0.70, 0.20, 0.10\}\)
  • Cross-Entropy: \(H(p, q_B) \approx 1.901\) nats
  • KL Divergence: \(D_{KL}(p \parallel q_B) = 1.901 - 0.897 = 1.004\) nats
  • Analysis: Huge information redundancy. This means due to Model B’s severe bias, every time it encodes weather information, it wastes over 1 nat of information just dealing with its own errors.

KL Divergence has two classic properties:

  1. : \(D_{KL}(p \parallel q) \neq D_{KL}(q \parallel p)\). KL divergence is not a true geometric “distance metric” (which requires symmetry). It measures the information lost when using \(q\) to approximate \(p\), taking \(p\) as the baseline.
  2. Non-negativity: \(D_{KL}(p \parallel q) \ge 0\). According to Jensen’s inequality, the divergence is 0 if and only if \(p=q\). This reflects a common-sense principle in information theory: no predictive encoding can be better than the true distribution itself. Any imperfect model will inevitably incur extra information costs.

Beyond Weather Forecasting: Applications in Machine Learning

The principles of entropy, cross-entropy, and KL divergence form a powerful “information-cost” framework that naturally extends into the core of modern machine learning, deep learning, and reinforcement learning.

Supervised Learning: In classic single-label classification, the ground-truth label is typically represented as a one-hot vector. For the correct class \(y^*\), the true probability is \(P(y^*|x) = 1\) and zero for all other classes, resulting in a true distribution entropy of \(H(P) = 0\). Consequently, the cross-entropy loss used to train classifiers reduces entirely to the negative log-likelihood:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}} = H(P, Q) = -\sum_{y} P(y|x) \log Q(y|x) = -\log Q(y^*|x). \]

Because the inherent entropy \(H(P) = 0\), minimizing the cross-entropy is mathematically equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \parallel Q)\)—essentially forcing the model’s predicted distribution \(Q\) to map perfectly to the true distribution \(P\).

Generative Models: In generative models like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma and Welling 2013), KL divergence acts directly as an optimization objective to regularize the latent variable distribution. VAEs are trained by maximizing the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), formulated as: \[ \mathrm{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_{z \sim Q_\phi(z|x)}[\log P_\theta(x|z)] - \beta \cdot D_{\mathrm{KL}}(Q_\phi(z|x) \| P(z)). \] The corresponding minimization loss is (_{} = -).

To reframe this as a minimization problem, the loss function becomes \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{VAE}} = -\mathrm{ELBO}\), which balances two critical components:

  • The Reconstruction Term: \(\mathbb{E}[\log P_\theta(x|z)]\) ensures the decoder can accurately reconstruct the real data \(x\) from the latent variable \(z\) (typically measured via mean squared error or cross-entropy).
  • The Regularization Term: \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(Q_\phi(z|x) \parallel P(z))\) forces the encoder’s output posterior distribution \(Q_\phi(z|x)\) to approximate a defined prior \(P(z)\) (such as a standard normal distribution \(\mathcal{N}(0, I)\)).

Knowledge Distillation: Knowledge distillation(Hinton et al. 2015) aims to transfer the rich representations embedded in a large “teacher model” (such as DeepSeek, GPT, or Gemini) into a lightweight “student model.” To ensure the student learns not only the hard labels (0/1) but also the nuanced inter-class similarities discovered by the teacher, we introduce KL divergence to align their soft labels:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{KD}} = \alpha \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}(y, q_s) + (1-\alpha) \cdot T^2 \cdot D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_t \| p_s), \] where the teacher’s and student’s smoothed probabilities at temperature \(T\) are defined as: \[ p_t = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{z_t}{T}\right), \quad p_s = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{z_s}{T}\right). \]

A higher temperature \(T\) yields a smoother distribution that reveals hidden inter-class relationships, while multiplying the term by \(T^2\) preserves the original gradient scale of the soft-label loss. By minimizing \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_t \parallel p_s)\), the student model is forced to replicate the teacher’s reasoning logic and predictive confidence. This yields a significantly stronger generalization capability than could be achieved by fitting hard labels alone.

Reinforcement Learning: In reinforcement learning (RL), KL divergence and entropy play distinct but equally critical roles in stabilizing training and promoting exploration.

  • KL divergence for constraining policy updates: In the clipped version of Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al. 2017), the objective function is: \[ \mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{CLIP}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ \min\left( r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t,\; \mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_t \right) \right], \] where \(r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(a_t|s_t)}\). By restricting the probability ratio \(r_t(\theta)\) to the interval \([1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]\), the clipping operation implicitly bounds the KL divergence \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}} \parallel \pi_\theta)\). This prevents drastic, destructive policy updates and ensures monotonic training stability.

  • Entropy for encouraging exploration: To prevent a policy from prematurely converging to a deterministic, suboptimal solution, many policy gradient methods incorporate an entropy regularization term into the loss:

    \[ \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_t\left[ \log \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t) \hat{A}_t \right] + \beta \cdot H(\pi_\theta(\cdot|s_t)), \]

Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al. 2018) advances this concept by establishing a formal “maximum entropy” RL framework. SAC maximizes a weighted sum of the expected return and the policy’s entropy by optimizing the following objective:\[J(\pi) = \sum_t \mathbb{E}_{(s_t, a_t)} \left[ r(s_t, a_t) + \alpha H(\pi(\cdot|s_t)) \right].\]This formulation structurally guarantees a superior balance between exploration and exploitation, leading to more robust policies with enhanced generalization across varied environments.

The Physical Roots: Thermodynamics and Statistical Mechanics

Before wrapping up, let us revisit the origins of entropy and KL divergence in their birthplace: thermodynamics and statistical physics.

The Physical picture: particles and energy levels: Imagine randomly throwing \(N\) small stones (analogous to independent particles) into \(M\) buckets (analogous to energy levels). The bucket each stone lands in is random, ultimately yielding an observed distribution where \(n_i\) is the number of stones in bucket \(i\). This process naturally generates a probability distribution where the proportion of stones in each bucket is \(p_i = n_i / N\).

Suppose we only care about the final macrostate—how many stones end up in each bucket, regardless of which specific stone went where. We describe this macrostate using a vector \(\mathbf{n} = \{n_1, n_2, \dots, n_M\}\) under the strict constraint that \(\sum n_i = N\). The question then becomes: How many microscopic arrangements (microstates) lead to this exact macrostate?

The first stone has \(N\) possible placements, the second \(N-1\), and so on, giving \(N!\) total permutations. However, because we do not care about the ordering of stones within the same bucket, we must divide out the redundant permutations by taking the factorial of each bucket’s occupation number. This gives us the total number of microstates (the multinomial coefficient) behind the macrostate:

\[W = \frac{N!}{\prod_{i=1}^{M} n_i!}\]

In physics, \(W\) is known as the multiplicity. Ludwig Boltzmann famously proposed that the physical entropy of a system is proportional to the logarithm of its multiplicity. Therefore, we can define the physical entropy per particle as:

If we further assume that each bucket has an equal prior probability (i.e., a uniform distribution with probability (1/M)), then the total probability of observing the macrostate () follows a standard multinomial distribution:

\[H = \frac{1}{N} \log W\]

If we assume each bucket has an equal prior probability of catching a stone (a uniform distribution with probability \(1/M\)), the total probability of observing the macrostate \(\mathbf{n}\) follows a standard multinomial distribution:

\[P(n_1, n_2, \dots, n_M) = W \cdot \left(\frac{1}{M}\right)^N\]

Taking the logarithm of this probability and dividing by \(N\), we obtain:

\[\frac{1}{N} \log P = \frac{1}{N} \log W + \log\!\left(\frac{1}{M}\right)\]

Because the second term is a fixed constant, we uncover a profound physical truth: finding the macrostate with the largest multiplicity \(W\) is mathematically equivalent to finding the state with the highest probability of occurring.

Uniform distribution to Shannon entropy: Now, let’s explore what happens in the macroscopic limit as \(N \to \infty\), while keeping the bucket ratios \(p_i = n_i/N\) fixed. Expanding our entropy equation \(H\):

\[H = \frac{1}{N} \log(N!) - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \log(n_i!)\]

By applying Stirling’s approximation (\(\log(x!) \approx x \log x - x\)) for large numbers, the equation transforms into:

\[ H \approx \frac{1}{N} \left( N \log N - N - \sum_{i=1}^{M} (n_i \log n_i - n_i) \right) \]

Given our constraint that \(\sum n_i = N\), the linear \(N\) terms cancel out, simplifying the expression to:

\[ H \approx \log N - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{M} n_i \log n_i \]

Next, we substitute \(n_i = N p_i\) back into the sum:

\[\begin{aligned} H &\approx \log N - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{M} (N p_i \log (N p_i)) \\ &= \log N - \sum_{i=1}^{M} p_i (\log N + \log p_i) \\ &= \log N - (\log N) \sum_{i=1}^{M} p_i - \sum_{i=1}^{M} p_i \log p_i \end{aligned}\]

Because the probabilities must sum to one (\(\sum p_i = 1\)), the \(\log N\) terms beautifully cancel each other out. Out of a classical physics thought experiment, we arrive directly at the formula for Shannon Information Entropy:

\[H = -\sum_{i=1}^{M} p_i \log p_i\]

From Non-uniform distribution to KL divergence: Now consider a twist: what if the inherent probabilities of a stone falling into each bucket are not equal? What if they follow a non-uniform prior distribution \(q = \{q_1, q_2, \dots, q_M\}\) dictated by natural laws or environmental biases? In this scenario, the total probability of observing the macro-event becomes:

\[ P(n_1, n_2, \dots, n_M) = W \prod_{i=1}^{M} (q_i)^{n_i} \]

If we take the logarithm of this new non-uniform probability, divide by \(N\), and apply the exact same Stirling approximation logic as before, the derivation yields:

\[ \frac{1}{N} \log P(n_1, n_2, \dots, n_M) \approx -\sum_{i=1}^{M} p_i \log\left(\frac{p_i}{q_i}\right) \] The right-hand side is instantly recognizable—it is precisely the negative Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, \(-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \parallel q)\).

We recognize the right-hand side as exactly the negative of the KL divergence, (-D_{}(p | q)).

The Takeaway: In the macroscopic limit, the logarithm of the probability of observing a specific macrostate is exactly the negative KL divergence between the observed distribution \(p\) and the prior distribution \(q\).

Bringing It Back to Machine Learning: This brings our story full circle. When we train a machine learning model by minimizing \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \parallel q)\), the underlying physical interpretation is identical. By optimizing the model parameters \(q\), we are actively transforming the probability of observing our training data \(p\) from an “extremely unlikely, rare outlier” into the “most natural, highest-probability typical event.”

This foundational logic governs variational inference, knowledge distillation, and policy constraints in reinforcement learning. It also explains why physicists without formal AI backgrounds often make rapid, groundbreaking contributions to machine learning—beneath the code and the compute, the two domains speak the exact same language at their core.

Conclusion

Since C. E. Shannon proposed the mathematical formulation of information entropy in 1948, this theoretical framework for quantifying uncertainty has laid the bedrock for information theory and statistical learning, continuously shaping the landscape of modern AI and ML.

From the basic cognitive bias in weather forecasting to the cross-entropy loss that guides parameter updates in deep learning; from probability approximations in VAEs and distribution matching in knowledge distillation, to KL divergence regularization used to constrain LLMs(Ouyang et al. 2022) and RL training—the “information cost” metric system built on entropy, cross-entropy, and KL divergence is more than just the underlying grammar of ML algorithms. It has become our core analytical tool for understanding and navigating a world of uncertainty.

Mastering this framework not only clarifies “what is being calculated” during model optimization, but more importantly, uncovers the fundamental logic of “why it is calculated this way.”
